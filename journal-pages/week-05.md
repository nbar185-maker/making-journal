## Documentation 

**Reflective Proposal**

*The experiments I worked on, especially designing the data protocol and the interactive data portrait, helped me understand how everyday data can be collected and translated into meaningful interactive forms that turn hidden patterns into visuals. This has shaped my direction towards exploring data physicalisation to make data more engaging, transparent because it shows the hidden impacts of human behaviour. Moving forward, I want to develop work that turns data into physical experiences that encourage people to think more critically about the environmental impact of everyday material consumption and waste. Similarly to my previous experimentation, I want the data to raise awareness and influence people’s behaviour by making the information feel more personal and relatable.*

*The public issue I have decided to explore is how waste fabric and scrap material data could be translated into a form students can interact with, making the consequences of material waste visible within their own design environment. This is important because textile waste generated through everyday design practices often goes unnoticed, despite contributing to larger environmental issues such as excessive landfill and inefficient material use. The project encourages greater awareness of material consumption and promotes more sustainable approaches*

*In a future university design space, a low-power AI system monitors fabric waste, categorising materials as reusable or disposable and suggesting reuse methods. This creates a system where materials are stored and made visible for reuse. Students still decide whether to reuse or discard them, but the system supports awareness and strengthens personal responsibility by making material impact more visible and easier to reflect on.*

*The data includes textile and material waste sourced from the ministry of environment, NZ Stats, and related sustainability reports. These sources are suitable because they provide reliable and large scale information on material waste in NZ. Additionally, observational data will be collected through the UOA design space to understand how students manage scraps in practice. This is suitable because it reflects students behaviour within the design environment and helps translate large statistics into everyday student experience.*


*The data is interpreted by highlighting how much fabric is wasted, materials that can be saved from ending up in landfills, and what types of materials are often wasted. Within the future scenario, the amount of materials can be measured before they are discarded. It shows how unintentional actions may create a gap between sustainable design practices.*

*It relates to datafication of everyday life experiences or behaviour, by turning everyday studio actions, like throwing away and sorting fabric scraps, into visible information. This makes routine behaviour easier to see, understand, and reflect on over time.*

*A shared board in the design space sorts fabric scraps by size and includes scrap-made artwork, making waste visible and encouraging students to reuse materials instead of sending them to landfill.*  

*This project provokes students to rethink the materials they treat as waste and to question whether these leftovers can still function as valuable resources that can be reused. By making waste visible within the design space, it highlights how material waste is actually handled, rather than remaining hidden or ignored.*

*I want my audience to better understand the environmental impacts of material and textile waste, and to recognise that these discarded materials still hold potential value and can be repurposed or reused by others instead of being sent to landfill.*
